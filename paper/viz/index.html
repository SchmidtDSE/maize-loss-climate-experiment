üåΩ Corn is in trouble.

Climate change threatens the future of corn. Studies expect planet-wide maize yields to drop by up to 24% by the end of this century (J√§germeyr et al. 2021). Why? Climate change alters growing conditions (Williams et al 2024), including a higher frequency and severity of stressful weather (Dai 2013) which can impact yields (Sinsawat et al 2004; Marouf et al 2013; Lobell et al 2020). Of course, these threats can impact farmer revenue (Sajid et al 2023). However, these changes can also challenge the institutions tasked with protecting and helping those growers (Hanrahan 2024).

What might this future look like and how can we prepare for it? This "explorable explanation" (Victor 2011) walks through an analysis of future maize yields from the perspective of one of these important institutions: the US Federal Crop Insurance Program. This crucial resource for farmers and the broader US food system is increasingly threatened by climate change (Ray et al 2015). In this online tool, we will go step by step through a machine learning / artifical intelligence model for this problem to understand and determine how we might respond to these threats.

Here's an outline of what we will explore:

  • We will predict the probability and severity of maize loss within the U.S. Corn Belt (NCEI) by simulating the Multiple Peril Crop Insurance Program, "the oldest and most common form of federal crop insurance" (Chite et al 2005).
  • We will use those results to see how insurer risk will change under the Yield Protection (YP) plan. This covers farmers in the event of yield losses due to an insured cause (Reuss).
  • We will contrast those simulations to an alternative which imagines a future where climate change stops today. This will let us quantitatively highlight the insurer-relevant effects of climate change in the 2030 and 2050 timeframes.
  • We will use these data to design possible policy changes to help mitigate and enable adaptation to the specific climate-fueled risks we observe in our results.
Our model will likely find that climate change will increase the rate at which farmers file insurance claims. However, we will also discover small tweaks to insurance formulas to include yield variance that could help mitigate these threats, benefitting both growers and insurers as they work to reduce the level of future crop loss and safeguard our food system.

Let's get started. You don't need to install any special software. We can do all of this from within your web browser. All that said, this online experience complements a more traditional scientific paper (preprint) which you may also review.

üìö First, some background.

Before predicting future yields, we have to get some background information out of the way both about crop insurance as well as the data and methods available for forecasting the future of the Federal Crop Insurance Program. Use the previous and next buttons below to navigate through some short slides to help orient yourself towards this problem.

If you are already are familiar with how US crop insurance works and what laws guide it, feel free to skip ahead a few slides.

Some terms:

(Slide 1 of 5) As with any government program, there are some acronyms and jargon. We recommend giving this a quick read before getting started but don't worry if you don't have all of these memorized. You can reference these definitions from within the text later.
  • MPCI: Multi-Peril Crop Insurance. Covering 494 million acres (Tsiboe and Turner 2023), this is the "oldest and most common form of federal crop insurance" (Chite et al 2005).
  • APH: Actual Production History. For our purposes, the average of the last ten years of crop yields (FCIC 2020). This has a number of different roles in insurance but is often used to estimate an expected or typical yield.
  • YP: Yield Protection. This coverage option within MPCI typically guarantees yield itself up to 75% of an APH (Osiensky 2021).
  • Insured Unit: A set of fields which are insured together in a policy (Munch 2024). For reference, this is sometimes also called a risk unit.
  • YP loss: The amount of crop loss covered by YP. For our purposes, this refers to when insurance begins paying out to a grower when the yields of their insured unit drop below 75% of that unit's APH (Osiensky 2021; Plastina and Johnson 2022).
  • Other loss: Other loss which may still incur a claim under other insurance plans. For our purposes, we assume a 15% loss threshold meaning it kicks in when yields drop below 85% of APH (Plastina and Johnson 2022).
  • 508h: Process by which new insurance products can be suggested. This could include supplemental policies covering more than 75% of yields (Schnitkey et al 2023) though typically programs cannot cover more than 85% as we will see in a moment.
  • US Corn Belt: A multi-state area within the United States that frequently grows corn (NCEI 2024).
In many cases, there's more nuance to these terms and programs than these short descriptions can capture. Still, these high level definitions are workable enough for the sake of our simulations. All that in mind, we are going to focus on MPCI for maize and YP loss, exploring policy changes either through 508h or by changing the law directly.

How rates are set:

(Slide 2 of 5) To understand how all of this comes together to set rates and coverage, we will walk through the structure of MPCI's YP option. A mixture of different information determines what price a grower pays for a certain amount of protection (Plastina and Edwards 2020). In other words, based on data about their insured unit, a grower can pay for different coverage levels that determine the YP loss. Generally, this involves county-level data, the APH for a insured unit, and a series of adjustment and subsidies. The producer can choose how much of their APH they want to insure (horizontal axis in the figure below) and, based on all of the factors, this sets a price (vertical axis in the figure below).

All that said, the horizontal axis in the figure below representing how much coverage a grower wants is currently defined in terms of a percent of APH. However, we will explore what happens if this includes an understanding of yield volatility. More specifically, standard deviation (Hargrave 2024) gets smaller as a grower's yields become more stable, offering a way to quanitfy risk. Including this variable in APH-based coverage levels could happen by 508h or by changing the law.

What US law says:

(Slide 3 of 5) It's worth mentioning that some of these structures are actually set by US law (CFR and USC). For example:
the level of coverage ... may be purchased at any level not to exceed 85 percent of the [expected] individual yield ... the yield for a crop shall be based on the actual production history for the crop
The structure of these laws are important and can dramatically change the outlook for both crop insurance and the farmers it serves. For example, we will explore what might happen if that definition were to change to this:
the level of coverage ... may be purchased at any level not to exceed 2.1 standard deviations below the [expected] individual yield ... the yield for a crop shall be based on the actual production history for the crop
We will come back to this question after we do some modeling.

How we will predict the future:

(Slide 4 of 5) We will have a neural network predict yields. It will be trained on historic growing conditions using data from a public resource called CHC-CMIP6 (Williams et al 2024) and the resulting maize yields as estimated by a model called SCYM (Lobell et al 2015). Then, we will use climate projections from CHC-CMIP6 (Williams et al 2024) to predict what future growing outcomes might look like. Finally, we will simulate YP across the US Corn Belt through a method called Monte Carlo (Kwiatkowski 2022).

Introduction to neural networks >>

We will use a form of AI called a neural network. Modeled after how biological brains work, there are different neurons connected to each other in a network that learn over time.

Artifical neural networks in a computer have multiple layers of neurons where, in our case, all neurons from one layer are connected to all the neurons in the following layer. The model learns by example: changing the connections between those neurons ("weights") based on "training" data. In other words, these models modify "neural pathways" based off of observing a series of inputs and their corresponding outputs.

We will dig into how we create these systems in the next step but the most important thing to understand is that we have a computer "learn" what types of outcomes are expected from what kinds of inputs by showing that system many examples in a "training" step. The challenge for the engineers and scientists constructing these models is figuring out how many neurons to use, in what structure, and how they should process examples. Regardless, this lets us estimate what future values might look like based on past observations.

For more information see the Nature of Code (Shiffman 2024).

Introduction to Monte Carlo >>

This modeling exercise is an example of dealing with unceratinty. In these kinds of systems, some variables have a range of values where sometimes all are equally likely and sometimes some in that range are more likely than others.

Let's imagine that we are evaluating the following equation for cost to purchase corn where unit cost ("c") is typically $2 but can be any value between $1 and $5 depending on the market. Meanwhile, the number of units needed ("n") can be any value between 50 and 100 based on the demand which changes day to day. The challenge is figuring out what the expenditure on the corn will be ("e").

e = c * n

In our model, we simiarly predict a range of possibilities instead of a single value. To solve this problem, we use a method called Monte Carlo such that, in each simulation, we randomly choose a single value for each variable from among its range of possibilities. Some values may be more likely to be chosen than others based on how likely they are predicted to be. Anyway, this method then repeats that process many thousands of times to arrive at a distribution of outcomes.

We use Monte Carlo beacuse it lets us find probabilities of different events happening. In our hypothetical, it could be the probability that the daily cost exceeds $160. In the model we will build together in later steps looking at insurance risk, this could the probability of YP loss found by looking at how often simulated yields were below the simulated APH for a simulated insured unit.

For more information, see a Monte Carlo tutorial (Kwiatkowski 2022).

‚ú® Next, a neural network.

Our neural network will learn from past growing conditions and outcomes (Williams et al 2024; Lobell et al 2015) in order to predict future yields within climate projections (Williams et al 2024). In this section, we will build that model but readers in a rush can skip to results.

More details about inputs, outputs, and climate variables >>

The inputs to the neural network include climate variables, year, and baseline yield (mean and std of all historic yields for the cell). The output is mean and std of changes to that future yield for the grid cell. Yield data come from SCYM (Lobell et al 2015). Historic growing conditions and future climate projections come from CHC-CMIP6 (Williams et al 2024). The climate variables are as follows (Williams et al 2024) where all are daily:

  • rhn: overall relative humidity
  • rhx: relative humidity peak
  • tmax: maximum temperature
  • tmin: minimum temperature
  • chirps: precipitation daily
  • svp: saturation vapor pressure
  • vpd: vapor pressure deficit
  • wbgt: wet bulb globe temperature
These daily climate values are summarized to min, max, mean, and std per month per grid cell before going to the neural network.

First, we will divide up the US Corn Belt into a geographic grid, breaking up the problem so it becomes tractable for modeling. Next, our network will go year by year, forecasting the yields for each cell in that grid. As yield may vary within even those small areas, the network will describe the range of expected yields as a mean and standard deviation per cell.

More details about the grid >>

We divide the US Corn Belt into small groups of fields to create a grid where each cell is about 28 by 20 kilometers each (Haugen 2020). This happens through four character geohashing, an algorithm that helps create these grids (Niemeyer 2008). Every piece of land ends up in exactly one grid cell but there may be more land dedicated to growing maize in some areas versus others. Therefore, our model will give more weight to a cell which has more corn compared to a cell with less.

Input data goes in one side and comes out the other as a prediction in our neural network. In the middle, a series of layers transforms those data where all neurons from one layer connect to all of those in the next. This is the part of the network that changes as the model learns from examples, altering pathways between neurons.

More about how we build a neural network >>

We are buliding a dense feed-forward model (Baheti 2021), often considered the simpliest kind of neural network. Usage spans 5 steps:

  • Prepare: Create the grid of cells on which the neural network will operate.
  • Split: Divide our data by year where our training set includes 1999 to 2012, our validation set includes 2014 and 2016, and our fully hidden test set includes 2013 and 2015 (Shah 2017).
  • Learn: Train a neural network by providing yield baselines (historic mean / std dev) and CHC-CMIP6 (Williams et al 2024) data on actual historic growing conditions and SCYM (Lobell et al 2015) estimations of yield going grid cell by grid cell.
  • Evaluate and retrain: Use the validation set to choose meta-parameters and retrain on all data.
  • Predict: Use that neural network to predict future yields in a predicted future climate (Williams et al 2024). This is a prediction of mean and std per 4 character geohash used for Monte Carlo.
Our scientific paper (preprint) further details the neural network including training as well as multiple different test sets for different kinds of operations that describe expected performance for different situations. Note that the model specifically estimates mean and std of percent change from APH for a grid cell, explaining why the meta-parameter interactive reports MAEs in percents. We use relu activation functions for hidden nodes (Maas et al 2013) with the Adam optimizer (Kingma 2014) and when we say "1 layer" we refer to one hidden layer.

The magic lies in helping the model understand if small fluctations in the data are real trends or just noise, a crucial task for which we have four tools:

  • Depth: The number of layers in the network (Brownlee 2020). Deeper networks can learn more sophisticated lessons from the data but can require more data to train (Brownlee 2019) otherwise it may memorize examples instead of learning broader trends (Brownlee 2020).
  • L2: A penalty on the model for very deep pathways between neurons (Oppermann 2020). Conceptually, this avoids focusing too much on small specifics in the data unless there is strong evidence for that connection. Turning this "knob" higher sets a higher bar for the evidence required for those deep connections.
  • Dropout: Randomly turn off neurons while training (Srivastava et al 2014). Conceptually, this encourages finding multiple ways to use inputs in making predictions and avoids relying on one small aspect of the data at risk of disrupting learning. This "knob" turned up means neurons are disabled with higher frequency.
  • Blocks: Sometimes certain data can be distracting to the model or simply having a large number of variables can become overwhelming (Karanam 2021). This option lets us take out individual climate variables.
This first interactive lets us try out different depths, L2s, dropouts, and blocks. Clicking the "Try Model" button will test a new model where we split the historic data such that the network trains from one piece (called the "training set") before we grade it on the part it has not seen before (called the "validation set"), scoring performance with mean absolute error. Our goal: go left on the horizontal axis (minimize error predicting mean) and to the bottom on the vertical axis (minimize error predicting std).
How to optimize model preformance >>

Optimizing the model requires a lot of experimentation. In general, we can follow some basic rules:

  • A large distance between training set and validation set MAE suggests the model pays too much attention to noise in the data (need to increase L2 / dropout or decrease depth).
  • Error too far to the upper right suggests underfit and may have to increase depth or decrease dropout / L2.
All that said, sometimes changing the parameters does not have the intended effect. Instead, data scientists building these models simply have to try a lot of different combinations.

More about why the sweep selected this configuration >>

Even with a computer trying hundreds of models, we still have to make some choices:

  • Do we favor the model's ability to predict the mean equally with its ability to predict standard deviation?
  • After chosing a model configuration, we retrain with all available data so do we want to emphasize a model with the best metrics in the validation set or one that has just slightly worse performance but less overfit so might do better with more examples to learn from?
  • Do we want a deeper model which might benefit from more data or a shallower model that seems to perform well in the smaller training dataset used in the sweep?
Sadly there is no one right answer but, in the selection we are using for the rest of this online lab, we prefer the ability to predict mean over std by a ratio in weights of 3 to 1. We also elect for a slightly deeper model in anticipation of retraining with all historic data.

üî≠ Let's explore what we predicted.

With our neural network built, the Monte Carlo discussed earlier can simulate what crop insurance outcomes might look like in the future. This lets us calculate loss frequency and severity, revealing how often we expect claims in the future.

Details about the simulations >>

In our Monte Carlo, we sample the neural network's outputs. However, to account for uncertainty caused by our model, we also draw from the measurements of errors we've seen from our neural network (Yanai 2010). Finally, we also account for the size of insured units (RMA 2024) since policies cover collections of fields and not geohashes or individual fields. Altogether, this paints a picture of changes to yield relative to APH at the 2030 and 2050 timeframes. For more information, see our scientific paper (preprint).

These data tell many different stories but consider a few different factors at play in these simulations:

  • Time: Even though APHs also change during this time in our simulation, in general things get worse in terms of losses as climate change progresses during the 2030 and then 2050 series.
  • Coverage levels: The picture may look different for policies insuring 75% of yield versus 85% of yield due to the way that the edges of the distribution or "tails" are changing. Those insuring at higher levels see some very high claims rates under climate change.
  • Unit size: The effects of climate change become less pronounced when simulating with the full insured unit where there may be more fields in a single policy compared to sub-unit which simulates closer to field-level. This is due to the "portfolio effect" where larger units offer more chances for high performing fields to offset fields with worse outcomes (Knight et al 2024).
  • Reference point: Things look different depending on what we compare against. Constrating the continued climate change scenario (SSP245) to "historic" values in the recent past sees average yields stay about the same but the risk of loss increase. On the other hand, comparing to the "counterfactual" (a simulated future where climate change stops today) has our neural network actually expecting yields to increase relative to SSP245.
Generally yields improve over time (Nielsen 2023) but climate change in our simulation wipes out most of the increases our neural network would otherwise expect if climate change stopped. This means that the average yield does not necessarily change dramatically from the historic APH but the ends of the distribution of yield outcomes or "tails" evolve. This represents less yield stability in the future such that the APH does not necessarily capture a risk increase even as the rate of claims for which YP pays out more than doubles in the 2050 series. This specific pairing of unchanged average yields but higher volatility is at the center of the risk posed to crop insurance. That in mind, we will next explore possible policy adaptations.

üìù How we might address these concerns?

We have a number of options to confront these increases in risk. For example, regenerative practices can reduce risks through diverse crop rotations (Bowles et al 2020) and improvements to soil health (Renwick et al 2021). This could provide output stability in addition to other environmental benefits (Hunt et al 2020).

Some options for confronting this risk >>

Though not an exhaustive list, here are some proposals from the literature:

  • Altering planting dates (Mangani et al 2023).
  • Physically moving operations (Butler and Huybers 2013).
  • Employing stress-resistant varieties (Tian et al 2023).
  • Modifying pesticide usage (Keronen et al 2023).
  • Adopting risk-mitigating regenerative farming systems (Renwick et al 2021).
As described later, the proposed policy changes do not necessarily mandate a specific option.

If we have the tools, what is the hold up? Recalling the intro, the average-based APH may play a part in the delay. Despite valuable resilience offered by regenerative agriculture (given that our results see higher claims not through overall reduced averages but higher volatility), these important practices may not always improve mean yields or can even come at the cost of a slightly reduced average (Deines et al 2023). This may be part of why, even though they guard against elevations in the probability of loss events (Renwick et al 2021), crop insurance may discourage these resilience-building steps (Wang et al 2021; Chemeris et al 2022).

Instead of insuring up to a percentage below average yield, what if instead one insures up to a certain number of standard deviations below average? This next interactive explores this question by looking at how different risk units might respond to this kind of structural shift.

More about the policy >>

Using recent data, the equivalent to insuring up to 25% below average is insuring up to 2.11 standard deviations below average. As shown in the interactive, this would increase under further climate change and stability worsens. All that said, here is how this would get formulated:

  • Average-based (current): loss = max(coverage percentage * average yield - actual yield, 0)
  • Std-based (proposed): loss = max(coverage standard deviations * average yield) / (yield standard deviation) - actual yield, 0)
Before moving on, there have been some issues of effectiveness of using crop insurance to encourage certain behaviors (Connor et al 2022). This situation may not offer a silver bullet. However, this method offers unique flexibility. While could help provide insurance incentive for practices like regenerative agriculture, any change which offers stability gets reward similar to how any practice improving yields operate today. Agnostic options may offer a possible non-prescriptive systems change.

Some of these changes may take place through 508h but recall that law sets some of these structures (CFR and USC). Therefore, some adaptation may require legislative action. In any case, we will next conclude our conversation by looking for other insights from these data.

üí° What does this all mean?

Briefly recapping what we've done so far:

  • We learned about crop insurance, specifically MPCI and YP.
  • We built a neural network which predicts distributions of future yields cell by cell and year by year across the US Corn Belt.
  • We ran simulations to discover that the loss (claims) rate will more than double at 2050 according to simulations. After wiping out yield improvements which would have happend otherwise, climate change leaves average yields relatively unchanged. Instead, the distribution "tails" change to show that yields will become less stable.
  • We examined coverage formulas. Since the current APH is based on a percentage of an average, it can be difficult for crop insurance to adjust to these threats. This same policy structure may also inhibit adoption of risk reducing practices such as regenerative agriculture. Incorporating variance into coverage formulas may help.
Though important, this is just one of the stories these data can tell and this final interactive encourages you to explore others. Note that this refers to statistically significant results to cut through noise but, if desired, one can turn off that filter.
More about the statistical tests >>

Around 91% of maize-growing acerage examined in this study see statistically significant results (p < 0.05 / n) in at least one year of the 2050 series. We determine this by a Bonferroni-corrected (Bonferroni 1935; McDonald 2014) Mann Whitney U (Mann and Whitney 1947; McDonald 2014) per neighborhood per year as variance may differ between the two expected and counterfactual sets (McDonald 2014).

Scientific abstract

To understand the impacts of climate change on agriculture, sophisticated predictive models build detailed projections from historic observations spanning many spatiotemporal variables. These crucial forecasts inform institutions such as government entities tasked with insuring grower livelihoods and supporting response to continued global warming. To help these decision makers engage these complex and often opaque predictive systems, our web-based open-source explorable explanation guides scientists, producers, policy makers, and other stakeholders through a data science pipeline in which an artificial neural network predicts future maize yields in the U.S. Corn Belt. Furthermore, after manipulating underlying assumptions and observing how changes impact model behavior, readers iteratively refine regulatory structures to confront a calamitous doubling in Yield Protection losses predicted at mid-century. Supported through game design principles of loop-based interaction and juxtaposition gratification, this experience not only explains AI mechanics and the specific shape of threats confronting the food system but also invites readers to experiment with different legal structures which may influence those outcomes. Altogether, supporting learning model mechanics before fostering play to refine regulation, these visualizations allow users to build understanding of crop insurance and predictive AI while supporting design of important policy structures needed for climate change adaptation.
