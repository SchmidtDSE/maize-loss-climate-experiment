Explorably Predicting Catastrophic Corn Yield Loss and Adaptation Potential under Climate Change

Summary: Using game design techniques, neural network-based "explorable" modeling highlights geographic distribution of increased catastrophic corn yield loss risk in the USA while also estimating adaptation potential for mitigation of these expected climate change-driven challenges in 2030 and 2050 time horizons, offering important insurer-relevant metrics as needed by regulators to confront these structural threats to financial and food security.
Introduction Methods Results Discussion Conclusion Works Cited Open Source

Introduction

90 million acres across the US grows corn, a massive amount of farmland not only critically serving the domestic food system but also the rest of the world (Ates 2023). Like agriculture more broadly, climate change threatens this essential crop (Gray 2021; Jagermeyr et al 2021) but, while tools exist for growers to adapt to these anticipated threats, this paper considers structural financial barriers to their adoption.

Prior work:

The literature anticipates a perilous 24% reduction in corn yields by 2030 (Jagermeyr et al 2021). To meet that challenge, prior work explores many mitigations from spatial adaptation (Butler and Huybers 2013) to machine learning predicting yield using weather, soil, management, and genotype (Chang et al 2023). However, despite modeling highlighting regenerative agriculture's role in climate crisis solutions (Bowles et al 2020) and mechanisms such 508h (Schnitkey et al 2023), prior work identifies crop insurance's current failure to "recognize the reduced risk of regenerative practices" as a barrier to adoption (McBride et al 2020).

Objectives:

This study projects future climate risk and adaptation potential through institution-relevant metrics to accomplish three goals:
  • Policy makers are experts who must craft regulation by conversing with data directly using their own knowledge Pottinger et al 2023) but the literature's necessarily complex methods are often opaque and published in static media that inhibit this "dialogue" and tell "the author's argument, and nothing else" (Victor 2011).
  • Though modeling often focuses on overall productivity (Jagermeyr et al 2021), insurers and regulators need statistically significant significant metrics regarding policy-relevant loss events in order to quanitfy benefits (Plastina and Johnson 2022).
  • Complementing prior work disecting individual threats and practices (Bowles et al 2020), this study considers multi-year climate simulations to attempt to reach closer towards more a necessary comprehensive understanding of "the full extent in production risk increases due to [climate] changes" (RMA 2022).
To offer this agency, relevance, and comprehensiveness, this study provides configurable interactive multi-year visualizations of insurer-relevant metrics.

Methods

This study affords interrogation of predicted yields under future growing conditions and this first explorable component explores model performance under different configurations.

Data:

This study trains neural networks using the following variables:
  • CHC-CMIP6's historic (1983 - 2016) and future (2030 and 2050) climate data (Williams et al 2023).
  • Soil Survey Geographic Database's yield expectation (Staff 2022).
  • SCYM's corn yields spanning 1999 - 2022 (Lobell et al 2015).
In the overlapping time period of 1999 - 2016, four character geohashes (Niemeyer 2008) create small geographic groups that aggregate daily climate data to per-month distributions which pair with non-longitudinal STATSGO2 data to predict longitudinal (yearly) yield.

Prediction:

A feed forward dense neural network predicts future yield per geohash. To optimize meta-parameters, this study sweeps architectures and reuglarization (Oppermann 2020). Models train on 1999 to 2012 data with (2013, 2015) held as validation and (2014, 2016) as hidden test sets (Shah 2017). Finally, fit to all available data, the final model generates future yield distributions per geohash for 2030 and 2050 and Monte Carlo estimates insurance event frequency in both a 1 year and, for comprehensiveness, 3 year trials (Kwiatkowski 2022). Though configurable, this study focuses on catastrophic loss (Tsiboe and Turner 2023).
Additional model details
The neural network uses soil and climate data as input to predict yield output in the form of mean and standard deviation. Note that all inputs and responses are z score normalized. Afterwards, Monte Carlo uses three draws:
  • Historic Scenario which samples historic yield distributions assuming historic climate.
  • Estimated Scenario which samples both predicted distributions under climate change and error residuals observed in model construction.
  • Adapted Scenario which modifies the Estimated Scenario by shifting predicted distribution to simulate adaptation.
Considered in three tasks:
  • Yield Task which compares Historic to Estimated Scenarios to indicate effects on average yields.
  • Un-Adapted Loss Task compares Historic to Estimated Scenarios to determine how often an insured loss event occurs.
  • Adapted Loss Task which compares Estimated to Adapted Scenarios to determine how often an insured loss event occurs.
Though reader-configurable, these results are filtered for statistical significance using the Bonferroni-corrected (McDonald 2014; Bonferroni 1935) Mann Whitney U (Mann and Whitney 1947).

Adaptation:

To determine relative potential for adaptation geographically, this paper observes that differences in yield between fields with similar climate and soil may arise from chance or practice and, thus, groups with larger variance may have more potential for adaptation than those with low variance. Though configurable in the Discussion section, this study estimates adaptation potential by moving results by one standard deviation per geohash.

Results

This work suggests relatively stable average production histories that, underscoring urgency for regulators and financial institutions, are punctuated by more frequent extreme losses with an average 48% relative increase in catastrophic loss probability compared to counterfactual. Responding to this concerning risk, localized variability thankfully also suggests that practices may offer mitigation, seeing an average 50% relative decrease in catastrophic loss probability. That said, both of these changes come with with geographic variation. This second interactive explores predicted yield, risk, and adaptation potential where, by comparing views, readers are encouraged to find groups of fields or regions that are 1) at climate risk and 2) may benefit from adaptation.

Details on selected model
From the sweep, this study uses a 4 layers deep network with 0.2 L2 regularization no dropout to balance the two responses and overfitting. This achieves the following performance:

The responses are z score normalized so these MAEs refer to number of standard deviations and reflect performance on assigning entire years to different sets. However, alternative schemes find similar performance. In a two split (train, test) set of supplemental experiments:

Discussion

This dual observation of stable average yields punctuated by potentially more severe losses may indicate a threat to the US food system and financial health of its crop insurance program, highlighting the need to leverage the adaptation potential highlighted by this study. However, before understanding those practical implications further in the Conclusion Section, discussion first turns to key parameters, future work, and design.

Parameters:

Results remain sensitive to geohash size and loss event threshold. First, though this paper focuses on the MPCI 25% catastrophic loss, different insurance products may consider others as afforded by this work. Second, 4 character geohashes afford strong performance but additional work may consider more granular predictions.

Individual practices:

This study attempts to understand adaptation potential geographically but specific regenerative practices such as crop rotation (Bowles et al 2020) and cover cropping (Deines et al 2023; Aglasan et al 2023) require individual investigation. This study may help those efforts understand climate risks but further practice-specific adaptation is left for future work.

Climate data:

The CHC-CMIP6 dataset continues to receive updates (Williams et al 2023) but, at time of writing, climate information cannot remains unavailable from 2017 to 2022 (Lobell et al 2015). Performance may improve as more recent climate data become available and this study observes may enable usage of more recent yield estimation methods such as the pending VAE-QDANN (Ma et al 2023).

Game design:

In crafting design of these "explorable explanations" (Victor 2011), note that this study analogies to mini-games (DellaFave 2014) with a series of small user experiences with distinct gameplay and objectives, allowing this study to reach the concepts of "gameplay loops" (Brazie 2024) and "juxtaposition gratification" (JM8 2024). These methods seek to offer deeper user agency and relevance.

Conclusion

Simulations see a volatile future needing to shift perspective from yield average to stability where the former moves little while the later probability of catastrophic events moves substantially. Presenting a particular challenge to both the food system and the financial structures supporting it: measures of baseline yields such as the USDA APH (Plastina and Edwards 2014) which use averages to manage risk may not change dramatically even as climate change increases the actual probability of loss each year, highlighting climate change's "silent" structural challenge in the food system and the financial solvency of insurance.
